Student Loans and Bankruptcy in Rhode Island

Student loan debt is a growing crisis. While discharging student loans in bankruptcy has historically been difficult, recent changes are making it more achievable for Rhode Island borrowers.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

The Changing Landscape of Student Loan Discharge

For decades, the conventional wisdom was that student loans could never be discharged in bankruptcy. That was never entirely true, and recent developments have shifted the landscape significantly:

  • November 2022: DOJ issued new guidance creating a more structured, less adversarial process for evaluating student loan discharge
  • Brunner reconsideration: Several circuit courts have reconsidered the rigid application of the Brunner test
  • Adversary proceedings: Filing an adversary proceeding (a lawsuit within your bankruptcy case) is required, but the process is becoming more straightforward
  • Partial discharge: Even if full discharge is not granted, courts can discharge a portion of student loan debt

The Undue Hardship Standard

Brunner Test (3 Prongs)

  1. Cannot maintain minimal standard of living if forced to repay the loan
  2. Hardship likely to persist for a significant portion of the repayment period
  3. Good faith effort to repay (applied for deferments, IDR plans, made payments when able)

Totality of Circumstances

Some courts use a more flexible test that considers all relevant factors:

  • Past, present, and future financial resources
  • Reasonable living expenses
  • Other relevant facts and circumstances
  • No rigid three-prong requirement

Alternatives to Bankruptcy for Student Loans

  • Income-Driven Repayment (IDR): Plans like SAVE, IBR, PAYE, and ICR cap payments at 5-20% of discretionary income. Remaining balance forgiven after 20-25 years.
  • Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF): Full forgiveness after 120 qualifying payments while working for government or non-profit employers.
  • Total and Permanent Disability (TPD): Full discharge if you have a qualifying disability.
  • Closed School Discharge: If your school closed while you were enrolled or shortly after.
  • Borrower Defense: If your school engaged in fraud or misrepresentation.

If none of these alternatives resolve your student loan burden, bankruptcy with an adversary proceeding for undue hardship is a viable path.

Filing in Rhode Island

Student loan adversary proceedings in Rhode Island are filed in the District of Rhode Island. The process involves:

  1. File your Chapter 7 or Chapter 13 bankruptcy case
  2. File a separate adversary proceeding (complaint) seeking discharge of student loans
  3. The DOJ or loan servicer responds
  4. Discovery, mediation, or trial may follow
  5. The court rules on whether undue hardship is established

Courthouse: U.S. Bankruptcy Court, 380 Westminster St, Providence, RI 02903

Legal assistance: Rhode Island Legal Services (rils.org) may provide guidance or representation for student loan adversary proceedings.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can student loans be discharged in bankruptcy?

Yes, but it is harder than other debts. Student loans can be discharged in bankruptcy by filing an adversary proceeding and proving undue hardship. Recent DOJ guidance (November 2022) has made this process more accessible, and some courts in the District of Rhode Island may apply a more flexible standard.

What is the Brunner test for student loans?

The Brunner test requires you to show: (1) you cannot maintain a minimal standard of living if forced to repay, (2) your financial situation is likely to persist for a significant portion of the repayment period, and (3) you have made good faith efforts to repay. Some courts use the totality of circumstances test instead, which is generally more flexible.

What did the DOJ change about student loan discharge?

In November 2022, the Department of Justice issued new guidance making it easier to discharge student loans in bankruptcy. The DOJ now considers factors like income, expenses, loan balance, and repayment history in a more holistic way, rather than applying the Brunner test rigidly.

What about income-driven repayment plans?

Income-driven repayment (IDR) plans like SAVE, IBR, PAYE, and ICR can reduce monthly payments to as low as $0 based on income. However, they do not eliminate the debt. Bankruptcy may be a better option if the total debt is unmanageable even with reduced payments.

Does filing bankruptcy stop student loan collection?

Yes. The automatic stay stops all collection activity, including wage garnishment for federal student loans, lawsuits, and collection calls. This protection lasts for the duration of your bankruptcy case.
