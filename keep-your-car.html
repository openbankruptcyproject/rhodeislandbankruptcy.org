Keep Your Car in Rhode Island Bankruptcy

One of the biggest fears people have about bankruptcy is losing their car. The good news: most people in Rhode Island keep their vehicles. Here is how car protection works in Rhode Island bankruptcy.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Rhode Island's Vehicle Exemption: $12,000

Rhode Island's $12,000 vehicle exemption is among the most generous in the nation. Most filers can protect their car entirely under this exemption.

How to calculate your equity: Take the fair market value of your car (check KBB or NADA) and subtract what you still owe on the loan. The result is your equity.

  • Example 1: Car worth $10,000, you owe $8,000 = $2,000 equity. Protected under the $12,000 exemption.
  • Example 2: Car worth $15,000, you owe $5,000 = $10,000 equity. Exceeds the exemption -- but options exist.
  • Example 3: Car worth $6,000, you owe $9,000 = negative equity. Fully protected (no equity for trustee to take).

Your Options for Keeping Your Car

Chapter 7 Options

  • Reaffirmation: Sign a new agreement to keep paying the loan. The car and loan survive your bankruptcy.
  • Redemption: Pay the car's current market value in one lump sum, even if you owe more.
  • Surrender: Give back the car and discharge the remaining debt if the car is not worth keeping.

Chapter 13 Options

  • Catch up on arrears: Spread missed payments over your 3-5 year plan while keeping the car.
  • Cramdown: If the loan is more than 910 days old, reduce the balance to the car's actual value.
  • Reduce interest rate: The court may lower your interest rate to a market rate.

The 910-Day Rule (Cramdown)

Under 11 U.S.C. section 1325(a), if you purchased your car more than 910 days (about 2.5 years) before filing Chapter 13, you can "cram down" the loan to the car's current value.

Example: You bought a car 3 years ago for $25,000. You still owe $18,000, but the car is now worth $12,000. Through a cramdown, you would only pay $12,000 through your plan (plus interest), and the remaining $6,000 is treated as unsecured debt.

If you purchased the car within the last 910 days, you must pay the full loan balance through your plan.

What About Repossession?

If you are behind on payments and facing repossession, filing bankruptcy triggers the automatic stay, which immediately stops repossession efforts.

  • Before repo: Filing stops the repossession from happening
  • After repo but before sale: You may be able to get the car back through bankruptcy
  • After the car is sold: Recovery is much more difficult, but you may be able to discharge the deficiency balance

If repossession is imminent, timing is critical. Contact an attorney or legal aid immediately.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep my car if I file bankruptcy in Rhode Island?

In most cases, yes. Rhode Island's vehicle exemption protects up to $12,000 of equity in your car. If your equity is below that amount, your car is fully protected in Chapter 7. In Chapter 13, you can keep your car while catching up on payments through your repayment plan.

What is Rhode Island's vehicle exemption?

Rhode Island allows you to exempt up to $12,000 of equity in a motor vehicle. Equity means the vehicle's market value minus what you owe on it. Rhode Island's $12,000 vehicle exemption is among the most generous in the nation. Most filers can protect their car entirely under this exemption.

What if my car equity exceeds the exemption?

If your car equity exceeds Rhode Island's $12,000 exemption, you have options: negotiate to pay the trustee the non-exempt amount, convert to Chapter 13 (where you keep property and pay through a plan), or use other available exemptions to cover the difference.

What is reaffirmation?

Reaffirmation is an agreement to continue paying a car loan after Chapter 7 bankruptcy. You keep the car and the loan, and the debt survives your discharge. This is common when you are current on payments and want to keep the car.

What is vehicle redemption in bankruptcy?

Redemption allows you to pay the current market value of the car in one lump sum, regardless of what you owe. If you owe $15,000 but the car is worth $8,000, you can pay $8,000 to keep it. This requires full payment at once (or through a redemption lender).

Can Chapter 13 help me keep my car?

Yes. Chapter 13 lets you catch up on missed payments over 3-5 years while keeping your vehicle. If the loan is more than 910 days old (about 2.5 years), you may be able to reduce the loan balance to the car's current value through a cramdown.
