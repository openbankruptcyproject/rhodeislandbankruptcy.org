Keep Your House in Rhode Island Bankruptcy

Your home is often your most important asset. Here is how Rhode Island's $500,000 homestead exemption and bankruptcy law work together to help you keep your house.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Rhode Island's Homestead Exemption: $500,000

Rhode Island offers a strong homestead exemption of up to $500,000 for your primary residence. This is one of the highest homestead exemptions in the country and protects most Rhode Island homeowners filing bankruptcy.

How to calculate your home equity: Take the fair market value of your home and subtract all mortgages and liens. The result is your equity.

  • Example 1: Home worth $250,000, mortgage balance $200,000 = $50,000 equity. Protected under Rhode Island's $500,000 exemption.
  • Example 2: Home worth $400,000, mortgage balance $150,000 = $250,000 equity. Check if this exceeds Rhode Island's exemption limit.
  • Example 3: Home worth $180,000, mortgage balance $200,000 = negative equity. Fully protected (no equity for trustee to take).

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13 for Your Home

Chapter 7

  • Homestead exemption protects equity up to $500,000
  • Eliminates unsecured debt, making mortgage payments more affordable
  • Does NOT stop foreclosure long-term -- only delays it during the case
  • You must stay current on mortgage payments to keep the home
  • Best if you are current on mortgage and need to eliminate other debts

Chapter 13

  • Stops foreclosure through the automatic stay
  • Catch up on missed payments over 3-5 years
  • May strip off underwater second mortgages
  • Protects home regardless of equity amount
  • Best if you are behind on mortgage payments

Foreclosure in Rhode Island

Rhode Island uses judicial foreclosure, which requires the lender to go through the court system. This process is slower, giving homeowners more time to explore options like bankruptcy or loan modification.

Timeline: Judicial foreclosure in Rhode Island typically takes 6-12 months, providing substantial time for homeowners to explore alternatives.

If you are facing foreclosure in Rhode Island, bankruptcy can stop the process. The automatic stay halts all foreclosure proceedings the moment you file. Chapter 13 then gives you a structured way to catch up on missed payments while keeping your home.

Lien Stripping in Chapter 13

If your home is "underwater" on the first mortgage (you owe more than the home is worth), Chapter 13 allows you to strip off junior liens like second mortgages and HELOCs. The junior lien is reclassified as unsecured debt and may be discharged at the end of your plan.

Example: Your home is worth $200,000. First mortgage: $210,000. Second mortgage: $50,000. Because the first mortgage exceeds the home's value, the $50,000 second mortgage can be stripped and treated as unsecured debt, potentially saving you $50,000.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep my house if I file bankruptcy in Rhode Island?

In most cases, yes. Rhode Island's homestead exemption protects up to $500,000 of equity in your home. If your equity is below that amount, your home is fully protected in Chapter 7. In Chapter 13, you keep your home while catching up on missed mortgage payments through a 3-5 year repayment plan.

What is Rhode Island's homestead exemption?

Rhode Island offers a strong homestead exemption of up to $500,000 for your primary residence. This is one of the highest homestead exemptions in the country and protects most Rhode Island homeowners filing bankruptcy.

Can Chapter 13 stop foreclosure in Rhode Island?

Yes. Filing Chapter 13 triggers the automatic stay, which immediately stops foreclosure proceedings. You can then catch up on missed mortgage payments over 3-5 years through your repayment plan while continuing to make regular monthly payments going forward.

How does foreclosure work in Rhode Island?

Rhode Island uses judicial foreclosure. Rhode Island uses judicial foreclosure, which requires the lender to go through the court system. This process is slower, giving homeowners more time to explore options like bankruptcy or loan modification.

What happens to my second mortgage in bankruptcy?

If your home is worth less than what you owe on the first mortgage, Chapter 13 may allow you to strip off (remove) the second mortgage entirely. The second mortgage is treated as unsecured debt. This is called lien stripping and can save homeowners thousands.
