Wage Garnishment and Bankruptcy in Rhode Island

If your paycheck is being garnished, filing bankruptcy can stop it immediately. Here is how wage garnishment works in Rhode Island and how bankruptcy provides relief.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Rhode Island Wage Garnishment Rules

Rhode Island follows the federal standard for wage garnishment: creditors can garnish the lesser of 25% of disposable earnings or the amount exceeding 30 times the federal minimum wage per week.

How garnishment works: A creditor must first sue you and win a judgment. Then they file a garnishment order with the court, which is served on your employer. Your employer is legally required to withhold the garnished amount from your paycheck.

Exceptions that do not require a judgment:

  • Federal student loan garnishment (up to 15% of disposable pay)
  • Child support and alimony
  • Federal and state tax debts

How Bankruptcy Stops Garnishment

Immediately Stopped

  • Credit card debt garnishment
  • Medical bill garnishment
  • Personal loan garnishment
  • Auto deficiency garnishment
  • Student loan garnishment
  • Most judgment garnishments

NOT Stopped

  • Child support withholding
  • Alimony withholding
  • Domestic support obligations

The automatic stay takes effect the instant you file your bankruptcy petition. Your attorney will notify your employer and the garnishing creditor. Garnishment must stop immediately -- any wages garnished after filing may need to be returned to you.

Recovering Previously Garnished Wages

Under 11 U.S.C. section 547, the bankruptcy trustee may be able to recover wages garnished within 90 days before your filing date if the total exceeds $600. These recovered funds become part of your bankruptcy estate and may be distributed to creditors or exempted by you.

This is another reason why timing your bankruptcy filing strategically matters. Consulting with an attorney or legal aid about the best time to file can maximize your recovery.

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13 for Garnishment

Chapter 7

  • Stops garnishment immediately
  • Discharges the underlying debt in 3-4 months
  • Garnishment cannot resume for discharged debts
  • Must pass the means test

Chapter 13

  • Stops garnishment immediately
  • Repay debts through a structured plan instead
  • Better for debts that cannot be discharged in Chapter 7
  • 3-5 year repayment period

Frequently Asked Questions

How much of my wages can be garnished in Rhode Island?

Rhode Island follows the federal standard for wage garnishment: creditors can garnish the lesser of 25% of disposable earnings or the amount exceeding 30 times the federal minimum wage per week.

Can bankruptcy stop wage garnishment?

Yes. Filing bankruptcy in Rhode Island triggers the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. section 362, which immediately stops all wage garnishment. Your employer will be notified and must stop withholding garnished wages.

Can I get back wages that were already garnished?

In some cases, yes. If wages were garnished within 90 days before filing and exceed $600, the bankruptcy trustee may be able to recover those funds as a preferential transfer. Discuss this with your attorney.

What debts can lead to wage garnishment?

In general, any creditor who obtains a court judgment can garnish wages. Common sources include credit card debt, medical bills, personal loans, and auto deficiency balances. Child support and tax debts can be garnished without a court judgment.

Does the automatic stay stop all types of garnishment in Rhode Island?

The automatic stay stops garnishment for most debts, but not for domestic support obligations (child support and alimony). Tax garnishment is also stopped temporarily. Student loan garnishment by the government is stopped during the bankruptcy case.
