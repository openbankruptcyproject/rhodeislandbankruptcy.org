Providence Means Test: Do You Qualify for Chapter 7?

The means test determines whether you can file Chapter 7 bankruptcy. Your income is compared to the Rhode Island median for your household size.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

If your annualized income is below the median for your household size, you pass the means test automatically and qualify for Chapter 7.

Step-by-Step Means Test Walkthrough

1Calculate your current monthly income (CMI)

Add up all income from all sources for the six full calendar months before filing. Divide by six for monthly average. Multiply by 12 for annualized figure.

2Determine your household size

Count everyone who lives in your household and depends on your income -- yourself, spouse, and dependent children.

3Compare to Rhode Island median income

If your annualized CMI is at or below the median, you pass. No further calculation needed.

4If above median: Calculate disposable income

Subtract IRS-approved living expenses, secured debt payments, and priority debts. If remaining disposable income (x60) is less than $9,075, you pass.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the median income for the means test in Rhode Island in 2026?

The 2026 Rhode Island median income thresholds are: 1-person household $63,147, 2-person $80,655, 3-person $97,449, 4-person $119,467. Add approximately $9,900 for each additional household member.

What happens if I fail the means test?

You can file Chapter 13 instead (no means test), challenge with special circumstances, wait until your income drops, or re-examine deductions with an attorney.

Does the means test apply to Chapter 13?

No, but your income level determines whether your Chapter 13 plan lasts 3 years (below median) or 5 years (above median).

What income counts for the means test?

Average monthly income from all sources over the six full calendar months before filing. Includes wages, business income, rental income, pensions. Social Security is excluded from the initial median comparison.
