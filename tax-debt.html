Tax Debt and Bankruptcy in Rhode Island

Tax debt can feel insurmountable, but bankruptcy may offer relief. Some tax debts can be discharged in bankruptcy if they meet specific requirements.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

The Tax Discharge Tests

To discharge federal income tax debt in bankruptcy, ALL of the following must be true:

  1. 3-Year Rule: The tax return was due (including extensions) more than 3 years before you filed bankruptcy
  2. 2-Year Rule: The tax return was actually filed more than 2 years before filing bankruptcy
  3. 240-Day Rule: The tax was assessed by the IRS more than 240 days before filing bankruptcy
  4. No fraud: The return was not fraudulent
  5. No willful evasion: You did not willfully attempt to evade the tax

Example: Your 2021 tax return was due April 15, 2022. If you file bankruptcy after April 15, 2025, the 3-year test is met. If you actually filed the return before April 2023, the 2-year test is also met. If the IRS assessed the tax before July 2024, the 240-day test is met.

What Taxes Cannot Be Discharged

  • Recent taxes: Tax debts that do not meet the 3-year/2-year/240-day tests
  • Payroll taxes: Trust fund taxes (employee withholding) are never dischargeable
  • Fraudulent returns: Taxes on returns filed with intent to defraud
  • Unfiled returns: Taxes for years where no return was filed (or filed late and less than 2 years ago)
  • Tax penalties: Some penalties may survive, depending on their nature and age
  • State taxes: Rhode Island state tax rules may differ from federal rules -- consult a tax professional

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13 for Tax Debt

Chapter 7

  • Can discharge qualifying tax debts entirely
  • Completed in 3-4 months
  • Non-qualifying tax debt survives the case
  • Tax liens survive even if the debt is discharged
  • Best if: your tax debt meets all discharge requirements

Chapter 13

  • Pay priority tax debts over 3-5 years
  • Stops penalties and interest during the plan
  • Automatic stay stops all IRS collection
  • Non-priority tax debts may be partially discharged
  • Best if: tax debt does not qualify for Chapter 7 discharge

Tax Liens and Bankruptcy

A critical distinction: even when the underlying tax debt is discharged, a recorded IRS tax lien survives bankruptcy. The lien remains attached to property you owned at the time of filing.

However, the personal obligation to pay is eliminated. This means the IRS cannot garnish your wages or levy your bank accounts, but the lien remains on existing property until it expires or is satisfied.

Strategy: If you have no significant assets, a surviving lien has little practical effect. If you do have property, Chapter 13 may be more effective because it allows you to pay the tax through your plan and clear the lien.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you discharge tax debt in bankruptcy?

Some tax debts can be discharged in bankruptcy, but it depends on the type of tax, when it was due, and whether returns were filed. Income taxes that meet specific age and filing requirements may be dischargeable.

What taxes can be discharged in bankruptcy?

Only income taxes may be dischargeable, and they must meet all of these tests: (1) the tax return was due more than 3 years ago, (2) the return was filed more than 2 years ago, (3) the tax was assessed more than 240 days ago, and (4) the return was not fraudulent and you did not willfully evade the tax.

What about tax liens?

Even if the underlying tax debt is discharged, a recorded tax lien survives bankruptcy and remains attached to your property. The lien stays until it expires or you pay it. However, you can negotiate with the IRS to remove the lien after discharge in some cases.

Can bankruptcy stop an IRS levy or garnishment?

Yes. The automatic stay immediately stops IRS levies, wage garnishment, and seizure of bank accounts or property. The IRS can continue auditing, but it cannot collect while the stay is in effect.

Should I file Chapter 7 or Chapter 13 for tax debt?

If your tax debt meets the discharge requirements, Chapter 7 can eliminate it entirely. If it does not qualify for discharge, Chapter 13 lets you pay it over 3-5 years without penalties and interest accumulating, and stops IRS collection actions during the plan.
