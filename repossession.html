Vehicle Repossession and Bankruptcy in Rhode Island

Is your car about to be repossessed? Bankruptcy can stop repossession immediately and may help you get your vehicle back. Here is how it works in Rhode Island.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Before Repossession: Filing Stops It

If you are behind on your car payments and facing repossession, filing bankruptcy triggers the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. section 362. The stay immediately prohibits the lender from:

  • Sending a tow truck to take your car
  • Hiring a repossession agent
  • Demanding you surrender the vehicle
  • Contacting you about the delinquent payments

The stay takes effect the instant your petition is filed -- even before the creditor is notified. However, you should inform the creditor as soon as possible to prevent a violation.

After Repossession: Your Options

If your car has already been repossessed, your options depend on whether it has been sold:

Car Not Yet Sold

  • Filing bankruptcy and requesting turnover of the vehicle may force the creditor to return it
  • Chapter 13 allows you to catch up on payments while keeping the car
  • You must act quickly -- creditors typically sell repossessed vehicles within 10-14 days

Car Already Sold

  • You generally cannot get the car back after it is sold
  • The deficiency balance (what you still owe after the sale) is unsecured debt
  • This deficiency can be discharged in Chapter 7 or Chapter 13

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13 After Repossession

Chapter 7

  • Discharges deficiency balance after repo
  • Cannot catch up on missed payments
  • May require surrendering the car if you still have it
  • Reaffirmation or redemption options available
  • $12,000 vehicle exemption protects equity

Chapter 13

  • Catch up on missed payments over 3-5 years
  • May recover vehicle after repo (if not yet sold)
  • Cramdown option if loan is over 910 days old
  • Keep the vehicle while repaying through plan
  • Better for keeping your car long-term

Rhode Island Vehicle Protection

Rhode Island's $12,000 vehicle exemption is among the most generous in the nation. Most filers can protect their car entirely under this exemption.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can bankruptcy stop repossession in Rhode Island?

Yes. Filing bankruptcy triggers the automatic stay, which immediately prohibits creditors from repossessing your vehicle. If repossession is imminent, an emergency bankruptcy filing can stop it.

Can I get my car back after repossession?

If the car has not been sold yet, filing bankruptcy may allow you to recover it. The automatic stay requires the creditor to return the vehicle in some jurisdictions. Once the car is sold, recovery is much more difficult.

What is a deficiency balance after repossession?

If your car is repossessed and sold for less than you owe, the remaining balance is called a deficiency. For example, if you owed $15,000 and the car sold for $10,000, the $5,000 deficiency is now unsecured debt. This deficiency is dischargeable in bankruptcy.

How does Rhode Island's vehicle exemption work with repossession?

Rhode Island protects up to $12,000 of equity in your vehicle. If you file Chapter 7, the exemption protects that amount of equity from the bankruptcy trustee. The exemption does not stop a secured creditor from repossessing the vehicle, but the automatic stay does.

Should I file Chapter 7 or Chapter 13 to keep my car?

Chapter 13 is usually better if you want to keep a car you are behind on payments for. It allows you to catch up on missed payments over 3-5 years. Chapter 7 can discharge the debt but may require surrendering the vehicle unless you reaffirm or redeem.
