Small Business Bankruptcy in Rhode Island

Running a small business in Rhode Island is hard enough without crushing debt. If your business is struggling, bankruptcy offers several paths -- from reorganization to an orderly wind-down.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Bankruptcy Options for Rhode Island Business Owners

Chapter 7 Liquidation

  • Business assets are sold to pay creditors
  • Remaining debts are discharged (for individuals)
  • Business ceases to exist
  • Personal guarantees can be discharged
  • Fastest option: 3-4 months
  • Best for: businesses with no viable future

Chapter 11 Subchapter V

  • Continue operating the business
  • Restructure debts over 3-5 years
  • No creditor committee (usually)
  • Debtor remains in control
  • Debt limit: $7.5 million
  • Best for: viable businesses needing debt relief

Chapter 13 (Sole Proprietors)

  • Available only to individuals (sole proprietors)
  • Continue operating the business
  • Restructure personal and business debts
  • Debt limits apply (currently ~$2.75M)
  • 3-5 year repayment plan
  • Best for: sole proprietors under the debt limits

Close + Personal Chapter 7

  • Shut down the business first
  • File personal Chapter 7 to discharge guarantees
  • Eliminates personal liability for business debts
  • Must pass the means test
  • Clean break: start fresh
  • Best for: sole proprietors with personal guarantees

Personal Guarantees: The Hidden Trap

Even if your business is an LLC or corporation, you may be personally liable for business debts if you signed personal guarantees. This is extremely common for:

  • Business leases (office, retail, equipment)
  • Business loans and lines of credit
  • Business credit cards
  • Vendor credit accounts

If your LLC or corporation cannot pay these debts, creditors will come after you personally. Filing personal bankruptcy can discharge these personal guarantee obligations.

Subchapter V: The Game-Changer for Small Business

The Small Business Reorganization Act of 2019 created Subchapter V of Chapter 11, dramatically simplifying the reorganization process for small businesses:

  • No creditors' committee: Saves significant legal costs
  • Trustee role: A Subchapter V trustee facilitates the case but does not take over operations
  • Faster timeline: Plan must be filed within 90 days
  • No absolute priority rule: Owners can retain equity without paying creditors in full
  • Consensual or nonconsensual plans: The court can confirm a plan even if some creditors object
  • Lower costs: Typically 50-75% less expensive than traditional Chapter 11

Cases are filed in the District of Rhode Island. Courthouse: U.S. Bankruptcy Court, 380 Westminster St, Providence, RI 02903

Frequently Asked Questions

What are my bankruptcy options as a small business owner in Rhode Island?

Small business owners in Rhode Island have several options: Chapter 7 (liquidation of business assets), Chapter 11 Subchapter V (streamlined reorganization for businesses with less than $7.5 million in debt), Chapter 13 (if you are a sole proprietor with debts under the limit), or simply closing the business and filing personal Chapter 7 to discharge personal guarantees.

What is Subchapter V of Chapter 11?

Subchapter V is a streamlined Chapter 11 process created by the Small Business Reorganization Act of 2019. It is designed for businesses with less than $7.5 million in non-contingent, liquidated debt. It is faster, cheaper, and more debtor-friendly than traditional Chapter 11.

Am I personally liable for business debts?

It depends on your business structure. Sole proprietors are personally liable for all business debts. LLC members and corporate shareholders are generally not personally liable unless they signed personal guarantees, pierced the corporate veil, or committed fraud. Personal guarantees are the most common source of personal liability.

Can I keep my business and file bankruptcy?

Yes. Chapter 11 Subchapter V and Chapter 13 (for sole proprietors) allow you to continue operating your business while reorganizing debts. The business remains under your control, and you propose a repayment plan.

What happens to my employees if I file business bankruptcy?

If you reorganize under Chapter 11 or Chapter 13, you can continue employing your staff. If you liquidate under Chapter 7, employees are typically let go. Unpaid wages are a priority claim in bankruptcy and are paid before general creditors.
